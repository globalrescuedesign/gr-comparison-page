Section 01

What Medical Evacuation & Rescue Coverage Includes

Not all "evacuation coverage" is the same. Understanding the difference between these core service types is essential before comparing providers — and especially before you need to use one.

Field rescue / extraction

Trained personnel — paramedics, military special operations veterans, or technical rescue teams — are dispatched to your actual location before any hospital or clinic is involved. This applies in remote wilderness, at altitude, at sea, or in conflict zones. Very few providers offer true field rescue. Most begin services only after you have reached a medical facility.[2]

Medical evacuation (medevac)

Coordinated transport — by air ambulance, commercial flight with medical escort, or ground ambulance — from a medical facility to a higher-level care center or your home country. Most providers define medevac as beginning once you are admitted to a local hospital. Uninsured evacuations cost between $25,000 and $300,000 depending on location and aircraft type.[29]

Nearest hospital vs. hospital of choice

"Nearest hospital" transport means you will be stabilized locally, potentially remaining abroad. "Hospital of choice" means your provider guarantees to move you to a specific hospital you select — typically in your home country — allowing continuity of care with your own physicians. Global Rescue (with upgrade), Medjet, AirMed, and SkyMed offer hospital-of-choice transport.[12]

Repatriation (medical & non-medical)

The return of a member to their home country. Medical repatriation applies when you require ongoing care. Non-medical repatriation covers return following security events, natural disasters, civil unrest, or the death of a travel companion. Most insurance-based providers cover medical repatriation only. Global Rescue covers both medical and non-medical events, including security-triggered departures.

Membership vs. insurance vs. credit card

Rescue & evacuation memberships (Global Rescue, Medjet, AirMed) send personnel to your location, do not require claims, deductibles, or co-pays, and can begin services before or without hospitalization. They do not reimburse trip costs or medical bills.

Travel insurance (Seven Corners, WorldTrips, Travelex, Travel Guard) reimburses trip cancellation, medical bills, and lost luggage. Evacuation coverage is amount-capped. Claims and documentation are required. Deductibles and co-pays typically apply. Personnel are not dispatched to you.

Credit card benefits (AMEX Platinum, Chase Sapphire Reserve, Capital One Venture X) are bundled with annual fees. Evacuation coverage is conditional and approval-based. You may need to pay upfront then file for reimbursement. Coverage limits are typically $100,000 or less. No field rescue. No security extraction.[26]

Common exclusions

Pre-existing medical conditions (varies by plan) · active war zones or declared conflict areas · extreme sports without an add-on · high-altitude rescue above 15,000 ft without an altitude add-on · injuries related to alcohol or substance use · self-inflicted injuries · mental health crises (most insurance plans) · travel against official government advisories · destinations under active travel bans · members over 85 (transport restrictions).

Section 05

Where Competitors Win

Global Rescue is the Best Overall pick in this comparison. But the right plan depends on your specific trip. There are six cases where a competitor is the better choice — stated plainly so you do not have to guess.

When to choose EA+ over Global Rescue

You are a senior traveler taking established, low-risk trips. You want a simple "get me home if I am hospitalized" guarantee at the lowest annual price. EA+ at ~$229/year has no age limits, no claims, and no medical exam — and you do not need field rescue, security extraction, or destination intelligence.

When to choose DAN over Global Rescue

You are primarily diving — recreational, technical, or as a marine professional. Dive-specific medical expertise, hyperbaric chamber coordination, and decompression treatment matter more than broad rescue. DAN is the only provider in this comparison built around dive medicine.

When to choose AirMed over Global Rescue

Your primary risk is a major illness or surgical complication abroad and you want the highest possible clinical quality of in-transit care. AirMed owns its fleet of medically equipped aircraft with ICU-trained crews — and you accept that coverage activates only after you are hospitalized.

When to choose Seven Corners over Global Rescue

You need a single product to cover trip cancellation, lost luggage, medical bills, and basic medevac — and you want maximum flexibility on plan length and traveler type. Seven Corners offers the widest mix of single-trip, annual, long-term, student, and expat insurance plans.

When to choose GeoBlue over Global Rescue

You are an expat or long-term traveler and your top priority is in-network access to high-quality international medical providers, with language-compatible referrals and Centers of Excellence — not rescue from remote terrain or active security events.

When to choose International SOS over Global Rescue

You are buying for a large organization — corporation, NGO, university — that needs enterprise-grade global medical and security infrastructure with on-the-ground presence in many countries, and you require a vendor whose entire model is built around institutional duty-of-care contracts.

5 questions to ask before you buy

1. Will this provider send someone to me, or do I need to get to them first? This is the most critical question. Most providers begin services only once you have reached a medical facility. If you are injured in a remote area without reliable transport, that is a meaningful gap. Ask explicitly: does this provider deploy personnel to the point of injury?

2. Do I need to file a claim or pay upfront? Direct-service providers (Global Rescue, Medjet, AirMed, EA+) arrange and cover costs without upfront payment or claims. Insurance-based providers require documentation and reimbursement after the fact. Know which model your plan uses before you need it.

3. Does it cover my specific activity and destination? Extreme sports, high-altitude mountaineering, dive travel, and travel to high-risk regions all have different coverage implications. Verify your specific activities and destinations are covered in the service terms — not just assumed.

4. Can I get home to my own hospital, or just the nearest one? Hospital-of-choice transport (Global Rescue, Medjet, AirMed) ensures continuity of care with your own physicians. Many plans cover transport to the nearest medically appropriate facility only. Confirm which applies.

5. What happens if my emergency is security-related, not medical? Most plans focus exclusively on medical emergencies. If you travel to regions with any elevated security risk, verify whether your provider includes security evacuation — and whether it is in-field extraction or a reimbursement benefit for defined events.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between travel insurance and a medical evacuation membership?

Travel insurance is a financial reimbursement product — it pays you back after a covered event, after you have filed a claim and provided documentation. A medical evacuation membership like Global Rescue is an operational service: it sends trained personnel to rescue you, coordinates evacuation, and transports you to the right hospital. No claims process, no deductibles, no prior authorization. Travel insurance requires you to reach a hospital before coverage activates. Global Rescue sends help from the point of injury or illness. The two products are complementary — most international travelers benefit from having both.

Does Global Rescue cover rescue from a mountain or remote wilderness?

Yes. Global Rescue provides field rescue from the point of illness or injury, including remote wilderness and mountain environments. Standard memberships cover most remote environments. For rescues above 15,000 feet — Everest, Denali, Aconcagua — the High-Altitude Evacuation Package is required (starting at $495 for individuals). No other provider in this comparison offers comparable technical rescue capability at extreme altitude.

Is AMEX Platinum or Chase Sapphire Reserve enough for medical evacuation?

Credit card benefits are conditional and limited. AMEX Platinum requires pre-approval for evacuation services arranged via the Premium Global Assist Hotline. Chase Sapphire Reserve provides up to $100,000 in evacuation but is often reimbursement-based if not pre-coordinated, and services begin from a medical facility rather than the field. Neither provides true field rescue or security extraction. For adventure travel, remote destinations, or any high-risk travel, a dedicated evacuation membership is strongly recommended in addition to card benefits.

How much does a medical evacuation cost without coverage?

An emergency medical evacuation by air ambulance can cost up to $300,000 depending on destination, aircraft type, and severity. A medevac helicopter costs approximately $60,000. Ground ambulance transport can run $5,000 or more for short distances in remote regions. A Global Rescue membership starting at $139 for a 7-day trip eliminates these costs for covered members. No claims, no deductibles, no upfront payment.

What does "field rescue" mean, and which providers offer it?

Field rescue means dispatching trained personnel to your actual location before a hospital is involved. Among the 16 providers reviewed here, Global Rescue is the only one that fully and consistently provides field rescue without preconditions. International SOS, AMEX Platinum, and WorldTrips offer limited or conditional field-adjacent response but do not deploy technical rescue teams to remote or extreme environments. All others begin services only after you have reached a medical facility.

Which provider is best for students studying abroad?

Global Rescue for comprehensive protection — dedicated student memberships for full-time students under 35, flexible durations (45–365 days), all 5 essential services. WorldTrips for affordable insurance-based coverage widely used in study abroad programs — reimbursement model, no rescue or security capability. For complete protection, the best setup is a Global Rescue student membership paired with a travel insurance policy covering medical expenses and trip interruption.

What does Global Rescue cost?

Memberships start at approximately $139 for a 7-day individual trip, $349 for 45 days, $455 for 90 days, and approximately $329 for an annual individual plan. Family plans, multi-year plans, student memberships, and enterprise plans are available. The High-Altitude Evacuation Package adds $495 (individual) or $995 (family). All services — field rescue, medevac, hospital-of-choice transport, 24/7 medical advisory, security extraction — are included in the membership cost with no additional claims, deductibles, or upfront costs for covered services.

What are the most common exclusions in rescue and evacuation plans?

Pre-existing medical conditions (varies by plan), active war zones or conflict areas, extreme sports without an add-on, mountaineering above 15,000 feet without an altitude package, injuries related to alcohol or substance use, self-inflicted injuries, mental health crises (most insurance plans), and travel against official government advisories. Global Rescue is notably broader than insurance-based providers. Always read the service terms — not just the marketing materials — before purchasing.

What is the difference between "nearest hospital" and "hospital of choice" transport?

"Nearest hospital" means you are stabilized locally — potentially remaining in a foreign healthcare system for your recovery. "Hospital of choice" means your provider transports you to a specific hospital you select, typically in your home country, allowing continuity of care with your own physicians. Global Rescue (with upgrade), Medjet, AirMed, and SkyMed all offer hospital-of-choice transport. Most insurance plans and credit card benefits cover only the nearest medically appropriate facility.

Do I need both travel insurance and an evacuation membership?

Yes, for most international travelers. Travel insurance covers financial losses — trip cancellation, lost luggage, medical expense reimbursement. An evacuation membership covers operational rescue — dispatching trained personnel to extract you and transport you to care. Global Rescue is not an insurance company and cannot reimburse trip costs or medical treatment expenses. The two products address different problems and work together to provide both immediate operational response and downstream financial protection.
