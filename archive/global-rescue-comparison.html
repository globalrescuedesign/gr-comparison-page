The Fundamentals

What Medical Evacuation & Rescue Coverage Includes

Not all "evacuation coverage" is the same. Understanding the difference between these core service types is essential before comparing providers — especially when an emergency actually happens.

🚁

Field Rescue / Extraction

The most demanding service tier: trained personnel (paramedics, military special operations veterans, or technical rescue teams) are dispatched to your actual location — before any hospital, clinic, or road is involved. This applies in remote wilderness, at altitude, at sea, or in conflict zones. Very few providers offer true field rescue; most begin services only after you've reached a medical facility.

✈️

Medical Evacuation (Medevac)

Coordinated transport — by air ambulance, commercial flight with medical escort, or ground ambulance — from a medical facility to a higher-level care center or your home country. Most providers define medevac as beginning once you are admitted to a local hospital. Costs for uninsured evacuations range from $25,000 to $300,000 depending on location and aircraft type.

🏥

Nearest vs. Best (Hospital of Choice)

"Nearest hospital" transport means you'll be stabilized locally and potentially remain abroad. "Hospital of choice" or "home hospital" transport means your provider guarantees to move you to a hospital you select — typically in your home country — allowing continuity of care with your own physicians. Global Rescue, Medjet, and AirMed all offer hospital-of-choice transport.

🔄

Repatriation (Medical & Non-Medical)

Repatriation is the return of a member to their home country. Medical repatriation applies when you require ongoing care. Non-medical repatriation covers return following security events, natural disasters, civil unrest, or the death of a travel companion. Most insurance-based providers cover medical repatriation only; Global Rescue covers both medical and non-medical events, including security-triggered departures.

Membership vs. Insurance vs. Credit Card Benefit

⭐ Most Operationally Complete
🛡️

Rescue & Evacuation Membership

Example: Global Rescue, Medjet, AirMed
  • ✓ Sends personnel to your location
  • ✓ No claims, deductibles, or co-pays
  • ✓ Direct service model — you call, they act
  • ✓ Services begin before or without hospitalization
  • × Does not reimburse trip costs or medical bills
  • × Not a substitute for travel or health insurance
📋

Travel Insurance

Example: Seven Corners, WorldTrips, Travelex
  • ✓ Reimburses trip cancellation, medical bills, lost luggage
  • ✓ Medical evacuation coverage (amount-capped)
  • ⚠ Requires claims filing and documentation
  • ⚠ Deductibles and co-pays typically apply
  • × Usually does not dispatch personnel to you
  • × Evacuation typically from a medical facility only
💳

Credit Card Benefit

Example: AMEX Platinum, Chase Sapphire Reserve
  • ✓ Included with premium card annual fee
  • ⚠ Evacuation coverage conditional & approval-based
  • ⚠ May require paying upfront and filing for reimbursement
  • ⚠ Coverage limits typically $100,000 or less
  • × No field rescue capability
  • × No security extraction or dedicated advisory

The Bottom Line on Credit Card Coverage

Premium credit cards can provide a meaningful safety net for routine international travel emergencies, particularly when services are pre-coordinated. However, they share three critical limitations: they do not dispatch trained personnel to your location, they do not provide security extraction from active threats, and their benefits are conditional on approval and coordination processes that may be difficult to navigate during an actual emergency. A medical evacuation membership like Global Rescue is an operational service that acts immediately — not a financial reimbursement product. For any traveler going to remote areas, high-risk destinations, or on adventure travel, a dedicated membership is strongly recommended in addition to card benefits.

5 Questions to Ask Before You Buy

1

Will this provider send someone to me, or do I need to get to them first?

This is the most critical distinction. If you're injured on a mountain trail, in a remote village, or in an area without reliable transport, "medevac from a hospital" is not useful until you reach one. Ask explicitly: does this provider deploy personnel to the point of injury? Only a handful of providers — led by Global Rescue — do this consistently.

2

Do I need to file a claim or pay upfront?

In a genuine emergency, navigating a claims process is the last thing you want to do. Direct-service providers (Global Rescue, Medjet, AirMed, EA+) arrange and cover costs without requiring upfront payment or claims. Insurance-based providers typically require documentation and reimbursement after the fact. Know which model your plan uses before you need it.

3

Does it cover my specific activity or destination?

Extreme sports, high-altitude mountaineering, dive travel, and travel to government-designated high-risk regions all have different coverage implications across providers. Some require add-ons, others exclude entirely. Verify that your activities and destinations are explicitly covered in the service terms — not just assumed.

4

Can I get home to my own hospital, or just the nearest one?

For ongoing conditions, long-term recoveries, or specialized care, being transported to a foreign hospital is very different from being taken home to your own care team. Hospital-of-choice transport (offered by Global Rescue, Medjet, and AirMed) ensures continuity of care. Confirm whether your provider includes or excludes this — and under what conditions it applies.

5

What happens if my emergency is security-related, not medical?

Most evacuation plans focus exclusively on medical emergencies. But a significant number of international emergencies involve political instability, civil unrest, natural disasters, or direct security threats. If you travel to regions with any elevated risk, verify whether your provider includes security evacuation — and whether it's true in-field extraction or just a reimbursement benefit for defined events.

Frequently Asked Questions

Your Questions Answered

The most common questions travelers ask about rescue coverage, medical evacuation, and how to compare providers — with complete, factual answers.

What is the difference between travel insurance and a medical evacuation membership?

▾

Travel insurance is primarily a financial reimbursement product — it pays you back after a covered event, typically after you've filed a claim and provided documentation. A medical evacuation membership like Global Rescue is an operational service: it actively sends trained personnel to rescue you, coordinates your evacuation, and transports you to the right hospital. Evacuation memberships have no claims process, no deductibles, and no prior authorization requirements. Travel insurance typically requires you to reach a hospital before coverage activates; Global Rescue sends help from the point of injury or illness. The two products are complementary — they cover different things — and most international travelers benefit from having both.

Does Global Rescue cover rescue from a mountain or remote wilderness area?

▾

Yes. Global Rescue provides field rescue from the point of illness or injury, including remote wilderness and mountain environments. This includes helicopter rescue, ground team deployment, and technical rescue operations. Standard Global Rescue memberships cover rescue in most remote environments without an altitude limit. However, for rescues specifically above 15,000 feet (4,600 meters) — such as Everest, Denali, or Aconcagua — the High-Altitude Evacuation Package add-on is required, starting at $495 for individuals. No other provider in the market offers comparable technical rescue capability at extreme altitude. If you plan to travel above 15,000 feet on any given trip, this add-on is strongly recommended.

Is my AMEX Platinum or Chase Sapphire Reserve card enough for medical evacuation?

▾

Credit card evacuation benefits are conditional and limited compared to a dedicated membership. AMEX Platinum offers medical evacuation coordination through its Global Assist Hotline but requires pre-approval, and all services are subject to eligibility review. Chase Sapphire Reserve provides up to $100,000 in evacuation coverage but is often reimbursement-based if not coordinated in advance, and services typically begin from a medical facility — not from the field. Neither card provides true field rescue or security extraction, and neither has 24/7 on-staff physician access. For routine international travel to established destinations, card benefits can supplement your protection. For adventure travel, remote destinations, or high-risk regions, a dedicated evacuation membership like Global Rescue is strongly recommended in addition to card benefits — not instead of them.

How much does a medical evacuation actually cost without coverage?

▾

An emergency medical evacuation by air ambulance can cost up to $300,000, depending on the destination, aircraft type, and severity of the situation. A medevac helicopter typically costs approximately $60,000. Ground ambulance transport can run $5,000 or more, even for relatively short distances in remote regions. In remote or international locations, these costs escalate significantly due to routing complexity, multi-leg transport requirements, and available aircraft. For context, a Global Rescue membership starting at $139 for a 7-day trip eliminates these potential six-figure costs for covered members. No claims, no deductibles, no upfront payment.

What does 'field rescue' mean, and which providers actually offer it?

▾

Field rescue means sending trained personnel — paramedics, nurses, or military special operations veterans — to the actual location of the emergency, before any hospital or clinic is involved. It is the most operationally demanding form of rescue and is only offered by a small number of providers. Among the 15 providers reviewed on this page, Global Rescue is the only one that fully and consistently delivers field rescue without preconditions. International SOS and AMEX Platinum offer limited or conditional field-adjacent response but do not deploy technical rescue teams to remote or extreme environments. All other providers in this comparison begin services only after you have reached a medical facility.

Which provider is best for students studying abroad?

▾

Global Rescue is the top-rated provider for students studying abroad. It offers dedicated student memberships for full-time students under 35, with flexible durations from 45 to 365 days, and delivers all five essential services: field rescue, medical evacuation, repatriation, 24/7 medical advisory, and security evacuation. This is particularly important for students traveling to unfamiliar or higher-risk environments for extended periods. WorldTrips (Atlas Travel) is the best insurance-based alternative, offering affordable and flexible plans widely used in study abroad programs, though it relies on a reimbursement model and lacks rescue and security capabilities. For comprehensive protection, the ideal setup for a student abroad is a Global Rescue membership plus a travel insurance policy that covers medical expenses and trip interruption.

What are the most common exclusions in rescue and evacuation plans?

▾

Common exclusions across rescue and evacuation providers include: pre-existing medical conditions (varies significantly by plan and provider), injuries sustained in active war zones or declared conflict areas (sometimes covered with add-ons), extreme sports without a specific endorsement, mountaineering above 15,000 feet without an altitude add-on, emergencies related to alcohol or drug use, self-inflicted injuries, mental health crises (most insurance plans), and travel against official government advisories. Some providers also exclude COVID-19, acts of terrorism in designated high-risk zones, and travel to sanctioned countries. Global Rescue is notably broader in its coverage compared to insurance-based providers and does not treat COVID-19 differently from other medical emergencies. Always read the specific service terms and member agreement — not just marketing materials — before purchasing any plan.

What is the difference between 'nearest hospital' and 'hospital of choice' transport?

▾

Most travel insurance plans and basic evacuation services will transport you only to the nearest appropriate medical facility — which may be a local hospital in a foreign country. 'Hospital of choice' or 'home hospital' transport means your provider guarantees to move you to a hospital you select — typically in your home country — once you are medically stable enough for transport. This distinction matters enormously in practice: being treated by unfamiliar physicians in a foreign healthcare system is very different from recovering at home with your own specialists. Global Rescue, Medjet, and AirMed all offer hospital-of-choice transport. Most insurance-based providers and credit card benefits do not offer this guarantee — they cover transport to the nearest medically appropriate facility only.

Do I need both travel insurance and a medical evacuation membership?

▾

Yes — for most international travelers, these are complementary products that cover different things. Travel insurance reimburses financial losses: trip cancellation, lost luggage, delayed flights, medical expense reimbursement, and accidental death. A medical evacuation membership like Global Rescue covers operational rescue and evacuation — dispatching trained personnel to extract you from an emergency and transport you to the right care, with no claims required. Global Rescue explicitly is not an insurance company and cannot reimburse trip costs or medical treatment expenses. The two products work together to provide both immediate operational response (Global Rescue) and downstream financial protection (travel insurance). Most experienced international travelers carry both.
